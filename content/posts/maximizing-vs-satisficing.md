## prequel

i just had a terrible friday.

well, if there was someone looking at what i was doing, they'd probably be like: what are you talking about??

i made good progress in my own ai safety work. i shipped 3 solid PRs at work, unblocked key pathways of pushing our feature from local dev environment to stage environment, did a smooth smog check for my car (except the mechanic crashed my car into a wall and popped the tire), called my girlfriend, facetimed my parents, finished everything by 8pm, and was ready for yet another perfect bedtime day.

but that day felt bad.

how???

i thought i didn't wake up early enough. i didn't end this task on time. i didn't get lunch perfectly at 12pm. i didn't have dinner perfectly at 6pm. i didn't get to do the full 90min call with my parents — instead it was only 30min. i didn't...

## body

in 2002, Barry Schwartz and colleagues published a paper titled [*Maximizing versus satisficing: Happiness is a matter of choice*](https://bschwartz.domains.swarthmore.edu/maximizing.pdf), in which they studied the differences between two categories of people: maximizers try to find the *best* outcome on every decision. satisficers settle for "good enough".

the theory comes from a perspective of "the amount of choice being examined" and "when do people settle with an option", as in their abstract:

> Can people feel worse off as the options they face increase? The present studies suggest that some people—maximizers—can.

however, in my situation, it's a slightly nuanced perspective: i'm subconsciously trying to maximize the "amount & quality of work done in a given period". i was trying to do a series of back-to-back-to-back perfect task sessions with zero buffer or slack in between. i was chasing the phantom of a "perfectly productive" day.

in their study, maximization showed negative correlations with happiness, optimism, self-esteem, and life satisfaction, and positive correlations with depression, perfectionism, and regret. in [another study](https://jsbs.scholasticahq.com/article/36755-when-good-enough-is-not-enough-the-role-of-maximizing-or-satisficing-decision-making-styles-innovation-behavior-and-entrepreneurial-alertness-in), the results show that maximizers are more perfectionistic, less optimistic, more neurotic, and more likely to procrastinate.

this area of study is super useful and points out two strategies that anyone can adopt:

1. pre-define a "good enough" criteria for a day → "if i get x done with x.1 and x.2 output, then it's a successful day"
2. instead of asking "what's the best time management technique for me?", ask "how do i structure my day/week so that completing things actually produces satisfaction?"
